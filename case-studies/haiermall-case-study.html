Redesigning the
Purchase Journey

HaierMall is the official Haier e-commerce platform for home appliances and consumer electronics a premium brand with 40 years of global equity selling high-consideration products in a competitive online market. Despite this brand authority, the existing experience lacked trust signals and a clear purchase journey, making it harder for customers to confidently evaluate and purchase products online.

01 — Context

The challenge, the approach,
the outcome

Every decision traced through a single thread: trust is a design variable, not a functional one.

Phase 1 constraint: No functionality changes. No new features, no checkout flow rewrites, no recommendation engines, no personalisation. Every interaction, backend flow, and data structure stayed untouched. The only lever was design — visual language, layout, hierarchy, component quality. How much conversion improvement can great design deliver without touching a single line of functional code?

Problem
A premium brand undermined by its own platform
40 years of global brand equity — invisible on the website. Black and white UI. No brand system. Navigation with zero hierarchy. Reviews buried below fold. Checkout visually detached from the rest of the site.
Approach
Redesign within hard constraints
Phase 1 prohibited all functionality changes. The only lever: visual language, layout, hierarchy, component quality. A full design system applied to optimised information architecture across every surface.
Outcome
31% sales increase in 3 months
Post-launch data confirmed the hypothesis: trust and clarity are conversion variables. Users cited ease, clarity, and trustworthiness as the three primary improvements — mapping exactly to Baymard's core conversion drivers.
02 — UX Audit

Five structural failures,
one shared root cause

Heuristic audit against Nielsen's 10 principles, competitive analysis across five premium appliance brands, and Baymard Institute research as the quantitative foundation.

Finding 01
Zero brand identity on platform
Entirely black and white. No Haier brand colours, no visual system, no premium signals. A consumer familiar with Haier from retail would land and find nothing confirming they were on the right platform.
Finding 02
Navigation with no hierarchy
All paths carried equal visual weight. Product discovery was failing early in the funnel — users couldn't orient themselves or determine which categories were relevant to their needs.
Finding 03
Purchase-confidence signals buried
Reviews sat below fold. CTAs competed with surrounding elements. The information hierarchy on product pages worked against how users actually make high-consideration appliance decisions.
Finding 04
Checkout visually detached
Payment pages looked completely disconnected from the rest of the site — a pattern Baymard directly links to checkout abandonment. The brand thread broke exactly where trust mattered most.
Finding 05
Mobile experience unoptimised
Below-spec tap targets, thumbnails too small for high-value appliance evaluation, navigation requiring horizontal scroll. Failed at the first mobile moment of product consideration.
Root Cause
A brand trust restoration problem
Every failure traced to the same origin: the visual design actively communicated the opposite of what the Haier brand represents. Solvable entirely within Phase 1 — because trust is a design variable, not a functional one.

04 — Design Decisions

Six decisions.
Each one a conversion decision.

Every design choice evaluated on its measurable impact on conversion potential, trust signals, or user orientation — not aesthetics alone.

01
Problem — zero brand recognition
Brand colour system applied end-to-end
Haier black as primary CTA colour, key navigational actions, and promotional signifiers. Critical application: checkout pages, previously disconnected from the rest of the site. An unbroken visual identity thread from first impression to order confirmation.
↑ Trust continuity across all touchpoints
02
Problem — flat navigation, no hierarchy
Mega menu with sticky header and weighted hierarchy
Existing navigation links retained exactly — Phase 1 constraint. What changed: their visual architecture. Primary appliance categories given highest visual weight. Sub-categories grouped. Promotional links visually distinguished. Sticky header maintained access during long product page scrolls.
↑ Product discoverability +40% faster
03
Problem — wrong information sequence
Product pages rebuilt around the purchase decision sequence
Layout restructured: hero imagery → price and key specs above fold → dominant CTA → featured reviews → detailed specs → upsells. Reviews elevated from below specs to above — based on Baymard's finding that 95% of users rely on reviews during product evaluation.
↑ Purchase confidence, reduced abandonment
04
Problem — checkout looked untrustworthy
Checkout trust signal overhaul within existing flow logic
Couldn't change checkout steps or logic. Changed everything the user sees. Baymard enclosed field treatment. Trust seals above the payment input. Persistent order summary. Full brand system through every checkout screen — eliminating the visual trust break at the most critical moment.
↑ Payment confidence — addresses 17% abandonment cause
05
Problem — mobile unusable for appliance evaluation
Mobile-first, WCAG 2.1 AA compliant component system
44px minimum tap targets. Single-column dominant product grids for major appliances — 2-column thumbnails were too small to evaluate product quality. 2-column retained for accessories. All colour contrast ratios verified against WCAG 2.1 AA.
↑ Mobile conversion, full accessibility compliance
06
Problem — cross-cultural handoff ambiguity
50+ component Figma system for zero-ambiguity dev handoff
Simultaneous handoff to Chinese and Pakistani development teams. Consistent component naming taxonomy. All interactive states documented: focus, error, loading, empty. Spacing in px and rem. Built to be extended by developers independently — no designer interpretation required at implementation.
↑ Implementation fidelity, reduced revision cycles

07 — Key Learnings

What this project taught about
high-consideration purchase design

Trust is a design variable
17% of checkout abandonment is caused by visual distrust — a design problem with a design solution. Visual credibility is purchase permission, and it's entirely within the designer's control regardless of functional constraints.
Information hierarchy is conversion architecture
Placing reviews above specs wasn't aesthetic preference — it was restructuring the page to match how users actually make appliance decisions. Layout is the product. Sequence is as consequential as the content itself.
Documentation is a design deliverable
Cross-cultural handoff revealed that naming intuitive to one team created ambiguity in another. A dedicated annotation layer should have been part of the design system from day one — not added mid-project.
Constraints sharpen the hypothesis
The prohibition on new functionality forced a precise question: how much conversion improvement can design deliver alone? The answer - 31% in 3 months — validates that great layout and hierarchy are not decoration. They are the product experience.
