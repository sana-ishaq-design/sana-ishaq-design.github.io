01 — The Challenge

A new platform. No prior
reputation. Trust built screen by screen.

Subscribe Me launched as Dubai's first digital-native car subscription platform — one all-inclusive monthly payment covering vehicle, insurance, maintenance, and delivery. The product was compelling. The design problem was foundational: users were being asked to commit a recurring monthly payment through an interface they had never seen before, from a brand with no established digital reputation.

Every design decision had to resolve one of three core anxieties before users could move forward.

Challenge 01
Pricing Transparency
Users couldn't parse which package covered what. "All-inclusive" meant different things across tiers, causing drop-off before users reached the booking flow.
Design response
Plan cards with full inclusion lists surfaced above fold. Every benefit visible before a user commits to evaluate.
Challenge 02
Booking Friction
High-value subscription checkout creates commitment anxiety. Multiple decision points without visible progress led to mid-flow abandonment.
Design response
3-stage named checkout with persistent progress indicator. Stage language chosen specifically to reduce anxiety at each commitment point.
Challenge 03
Ongoing Management
After subscribing, users needed to manage services, request support, and handle vehicle issues — all through a platform with no existing operational interface.
Design response
Centralised dashboard combining bookings, services, documents, and support into a single management hub.

What the reviews confirm — design working

Positive reviews cite ease of use and a seamless booking journey. No reviewer complains about navigation confusion, unclear pricing, or a broken checkout — the three problems the design was built to solve.

What the reviews flag — next design frontier

Critical reviews identify operational failures — car swaps, deposit delays, unreachable support. These are service problems, not interface problems. Version 2's design scope: in-app dispute resolution and transparent billing history.

07 — Reflection

What this project reinforced
about designing under pressure.

Design system literacy is as important as design skill

Joining at Sprint 4 demanded reading another designer's decisions and extending them faithfully. I now build a system-reading session into every project I join — regardless of whether I'm starting from scratch or inheriting existing work.

Evidence turns a preference into a recommendation

Defending the checkout progress bar in a stakeholder meeting taught me the difference between a design conversation and a design negotiation. Stakeholders can override opinions. Research is harder to dismiss.

Design doesn't end at handoff

The post-launch UI audit found cumulative visual drift — small decisions under deadline pressure, consequential in aggregate. A design system is only as valuable as its implementation.

The interface is the brand — when there's no other brand to lean on

For a new platform with no prior reputation, visual design quality was the primary trust proxy. Designing for trust through pricing clarity, commitment transparency, and visual consistency isn't a UX concern. It's a business one.

Closing thought

This project challenged me to balance complex subscription workflows with simplicity. Designing both the customer journey and the underlying design system reinforced the importance of scalable patterns, clear information architecture, and outcome-focused product design — within real professional constraints.
