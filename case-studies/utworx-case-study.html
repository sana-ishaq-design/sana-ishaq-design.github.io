01 — Project Overview

UTWorX serves maintenance
professionals who can't afford
to lose time in the interface.

UTWorX is an enterprise SaaS platform for asset and maintenance management — helping facility managers, maintenance supervisors, technicians, and schedulers track assets, manage work orders, schedule preventive maintenance, and monitor operational performance. As the platform's feature set grew, its interface hadn't kept pace. Increasingly powerful functionality was trapped behind an increasingly difficult-to-navigate design.

The product team's brief was precise: make the platform easier to use without changing what it does. The redesign had to serve three distinct user roles simultaneously — each with different information needs, different frequency of use, and different levels of time pressure.

Maintenance Managers Facility Managers Technicians (PICs) Operations Teams Asset Owners
Operators / Technicians
On-site execution
Receive fault notifications, prioritise tasks, log completion — often on mobile while physically on-site. Every extra tap costs real time.
Supervisors / Team Leads
Coordination layer
Delegate tasks, track team progress, surface blockers in real time. Were losing visibility when updates got buried in unprioritised tables.
Managers / Schedulers
Strategic oversight
Need KPIs at a glance — asset performance, completion rates, spare part consumption. Were spending too long assembling this picture from scattered screens.

02 — The Challenge

Five audit failures.
One root cause.

Before designing anything, a structured UX audit of the existing system revealed a consistent pattern. Every problem traced to the same structural failure: the platform was built around how data is stored, not around how users need to act on it.

Challenge 01
⚡
Information Overload
Raw data tables dominated every screen. Fault notifications arrived without priority signals — operators had to read every record in full to identify urgent tasks.
Challenge 02
🧭
Difficult Navigation
Building an operational picture required 4–5 separate screens. Work order creation caused mid-flow abandonment. Supervisors coordinated off-platform via phone and chat.
Challenge 03
📊
No Dashboard Visibility
The platform held all the data managers needed — but it was distributed with no visual synthesis. Time-sensitive decisions were made on incomplete information.
The reframed problem

UTWorX users are failing not because they lack information, but because the interface requires too much cognitive effort to extract actionable insight. The redesign must transform how information is presented — surfacing priorities, reducing navigation depth — without reducing data completeness or functional scope.

03 — Design Goals

Four principles.
Every screen evaluated
against all four.

◈
Simplify Data
Present the same information through visual summaries rather than raw tables. Same depth, lower cognitive load.
◉
Improve Visibility
KPIs and operational status immediately readable from the dashboard without opening sub-screens.
◐
Streamline Workflows
Reduce interactions for core tasks. Contextual modals over dedicated pages. Guided flows over open forms.
◑
Modernize Experience
Enterprise-appropriate visual system that communicates reliability and reduces retraining cost through familiar patterns.

04 — Dashboard Redesign

Transforming data tables
into actionable insights.

The previous system relied on raw data tables. The redesign introduced visual summaries, KPI cards, and charts to help users understand system performance at a glance. Managers can now answer critical operational questions — completion rates, asset status, spend trends — without opening a single sub-screen.

→Asset Overview: Live asset status — active, pending, faulted — surfaced as a summary card above all tables
→Work Order Performance: Weekly, monthly, and yearly completion trends as line charts — replacing tabular history logs
→Spare Part Consumption: Stacked bar chart showing composition across categories — patterns invisible in raw tables become immediately visible
→Maintenance Trends: Proactive scheduling pipeline — what's due, what's overdue, what's at risk

05 — Work Order Management

Streamlining maintenance
workflows.

Work order creation was redesigned as a named 5-step structured flow. Each step is scoped to its specific information requirements — no step asks for what belongs to another stage. Progress is persistent and visible throughout, making the process feel finite and bounded rather than open-ended.

Preliminary Report

Step 03

Step 04

Step 05

→Priority Signals: Colour-coded severity indicators allow operators to triage fault urgency without reading every record in full
→Contextual Modals: Secondary actions (adding spare parts, uploading documents, assigning personnel) handled in-context — no page transitions
→Persistent Progress Bar: Named steps visible throughout — users always know where they are and how far remains in the workflow

06 — Preventive Maintenance

Designing for
operational efficiency at scale.

The PM scheduler was rebuilt around a spreadsheet-style bulk management interface — allowing schedulers to assign personnel, define tasks, track spare parts, and set recurrence rules across multiple assets simultaneously. For facilities managing large equipment portfolios, this reduces scheduling from a multi-hour manual process to a systematic workflow.

→Assign PIC: Personnel assignment directly in-line — no modal or separate screen required
→Task Details: Recurrence rules, task type, and asset linkage managed in a single row
→Spare Parts Tracking: Parts consumption logged at the task level, building records during the process rather than retroactively
→Completion Notes: Documentation upload and completion sign-off integrated at the task level

The spreadsheet-style bulk upload interface reflects how maintenance schedulers actually think — in rows and columns when managing many tasks simultaneously. A card-based approach would have required too many interactions at scale.

07 — Reporting & Analytics

Making reporting
faster and easier.

The reporting section was redesigned to support date-based, asset-specific, and status-filtered report generation — replacing the single long-scroll table with a structured filter-first interface that makes report boundaries explicit before the data loads.

→Filters Panel: Report scope defined upfront — asset type, date range, status — before data loads
→Asset Tracking: Filter by asset ID, location, or category for targeted maintenance history reports
→Work Status: Status-based filtering surfaces only open, completed, or overdue records as needed
→Date-Based Reports: Weekly, monthly, or custom date range selection for period-specific compliance reporting

08 — Key Design Decisions

Four decisions.
Each with an alternative
I chose not to take.

DecisionAlternative consideredChoice & rationale
Navigation architecture Top navigation bar — maximises vertical content space, familiar from consumer web Sidebar chosen Enterprise users work within the same tool for hours across many modules. A sidebar scales cleanly as module count grows and matches familiar enterprise patterns (Salesforce, ServiceNow) — reducing retraining cost.
Data on dashboard Improved tables — better sorting, filtering, colour-coded rows. Lower design risk. Charts + visual summaries chosen Tables require users to read and compare rows to extract trends. Charts communicate the same information as pre-processed visuals. For managers assessing operational health quickly, charts deliver insight in seconds that tables require minutes to extract.
Work order creation Single long-form page — all fields on one screen, user scrolls to complete. 5-step structured workflow chosen Work order creation is cognitively complex — fault details, asset assignment, scheduling, personnel, and documentation each require focused attention. A structured flow scopes the user's attention at each stage, reducing errors and creating consistent, complete records.
Secondary actions Dedicated pages — every secondary action navigates to a separate full screen. Contextual modals chosen Dedicated pages break user context — the user leaves, completes the action, returns. Modals handle secondary actions within the context of the primary workflow. For a user completing ten work orders in a session, avoiding twenty unnecessary page transitions matters at scale.

10 — Design Impact

What the redesign
delivered.

Formal usability metrics were not collected within the two-week scope. The outcomes reported by the product team and client stakeholders post-delivery:

◈
Cleaner Interface
Visual design reduced perceived complexity without removing any data or functionality from the platform
◉
Modern Experience
Updated visual system aligned the platform with enterprise SaaS standards users recognise from other tools
◐
Easier Navigation
Sidebar architecture and structured flows reduced navigation depth for all three user roles
◑
Improved Workflow Efficiency
Task creation, scheduling, and reporting workflows required fewer interactions and less off-platform coordination

11 — Key Learnings

What enterprise design
confirmed.

Learning 01
Simplifying data is often more valuable than reducing it
The same data, structured for the decision it needs to support, can feel dramatically simpler without losing any of its depth. Complexity is often a presentation failure, not a content failure.
Learning 02
Enterprise users prioritise efficiency over novelty
In enterprise tools, familiarity is a feature. Borrowing from established patterns (Salesforce, ServiceNow) reduced retraining cost. The creativity in enterprise UX is applying familiar patterns with enough precision that the interface disappears and the work comes forward.
Learning 03
Visual hierarchy improves decision-making speed
Colour-coded priority signals, dashboard chart types matched to insight type, and contextual information surfacing at the point of need — each one reduced the cognitive effort required to act on information under time pressure.

12 — Reflection

What I'd do
differently.

Closing thought

The two-week timeline made formal user testing impossible — and that's the honest gap in this project. In retrospect, a single half-day session with three to five current users testing the work order creation and PM scheduler flows would have given me evidence to replace reasonable assumptions. The redesign addressed the presentation layer effectively. What it didn't address — because it was out of scope — is the intelligence layer: predictive maintenance alerts, proactive scheduling suggestions, and anomaly detection. The data to support this already exists in the platform. Version 2's design work is making that predictive capability visible and actionable.
